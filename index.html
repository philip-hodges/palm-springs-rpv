Ecological Regression
Non-White CVAP % vs. Vote Share — 5 districts per race
Palm Springs (Actual)
What RPV Looks Like

Standard RPV methodology: regress vote share on racial composition. Steep slope = polarization.

Finding: The regression line is essentially flat (slope = -0.026). Racial composition explains 0.03% of the variance in vote share (R² = 0.0003). In successful RPV cases, plaintiffs typically show R² of 0.50–0.90. The slight negative slope means more-minority districts are marginally less progressive — the opposite of an RPV finding.

Palm Springs (Actual)
What RPV Looks Like
Finding: District 1 (51.5% non-white CVAP) and the rest of Palm Springs (avg 22.9% non-white CVAP) vote within ±0.96 pts of each other on average. Correlation: r = 0.9984. The demographics vary enormously; the voting does not.
Distribution of Voting Gaps (D1 minus D2–5)
Positive = D1 more progressive. Negative = D1 less progressive. Sorted by gap size.
Finding: Mean gap = -0.42 pts (std = 1.17). The direction is opposite to RPV prediction. Cohen's d = -0.36 (small/negligible effect). No race exceeds a ±3 point gap. All 38 gaps cluster near zero.
June 2022 Primary: Within-Party Test
If RPV exists, D1 should prefer different candidates even within the same party. Does it?
Finding: Even when partisanship is completely removed — comparing Democrat vs. Democrat and Republican vs. Republican — District 1 votes within 0.6–4.4 pts of the rest of the city. All gaps are small, and all go in the same direction (D1 slightly less frontrunner-favoring). This is normal variation, not a racial signal.
